Motivational Statement

The Emerging Voices for Global Health (EV4GH) 2026 – Track 2 is more than just a training program; it's a forum for turning real-world research experiences into impactful global discussions. In my capacity as a Nigerian public health researcher working at the nexus of infectious diseases, gender, and health system reinforcement, I have observed how evidence produced locally frequently has difficulty making its way to decision-making tables. The link between meticulous research and fair representation in discussions about global health is exemplified by EV4GH. EV4GH stands for voice, visibility, and validation, particularly for scholars conducting research in underrepresented communities. It demonstrates a dedication to changing the dynamics of global health by highlighting viewpoints from low- and middle-income nations. As a member of this network, I would be able to improve how I convey complicated research results into engaging stories that have an impact on policy and practice.

The will help me improve my abilities in strategic networking, policy participation, and scientific communication in my professional life. I want to improve my ability to turn research on integrated disease screening and health equity into effective advocacy messages by using peer learning and mentorship. The chance to interact with a varied group of rising leaders will help me expand my viewpoint and develop lasting relationships.

In the end, EV4GH will provide me with the tools to influence discussions and provide evidence, making sure that the research I do in my environment makes a significant contribution to the global movement for health equity.

Short Concept Note

Development of an Integrated Framework for Screening and Policy Response to Female Genital Schistosomiasis, HIV, and Cervical Cancer in Nigeria

Female reproductive health in many low- and middle-income countries is affected by overlapping infectious and non-communicable conditions that remain poorly integrated within health systems. In Nigeria, Female Genital Schistosomiasis (FGS)—a neglected manifestation of Schistosomiasis—is increasingly recognized as a major but underdiagnosed condition among women and girls exposed to infested water bodies. Evidence shows that FGS may increase vulnerability to HIV/AIDS infection and may mimic or coexist with symptoms of Cervical Cancer, creating diagnostic and management challenges. Despite these intersections, current health services in Nigeria address these conditions through fragmented programs with limited coordination across disease control, reproductive health, and policy structures.

This gap reflects a broader global health challenge: the lack of integrated approaches to diseases that intersect biologically, socially, and programmatically. The absence of clear screening guidelines for FGS within routine reproductive health services further contributes to misdiagnosis, underreporting, and missed opportunities for early treatment and prevention.

This concept proposes the development of an integrated framework for screening and policy response to FGS, HIV, and cervical cancer in Nigeria. The framework will aim to bridge clinical, public health, and policy gaps by identifying feasible pathways for integrating FGS detection into existing HIV and cervical cancer screening services, particularly at primary healthcare levels.

The project will involve three key components. First, a situational analysis will assess current policies, service delivery models, and health system readiness for integrated screening, including the perspectives of healthcare workers, policymakers, and affected communities. Second, evidence from epidemiological data and stakeholder engagement will be synthesized to design a practical screening algorithm that links FGS identification with HIV testing and cervical cancer screening within reproductive health services. Third, the study will develop policy recommendations and an operational framework to guide integration into national disease control and sexual and reproductive health programs.

Aligned with the goals of the Emerging Voices for Global Health (EV4GH) program, this work seeks to amplify locally generated evidence and promote more equitable global health discourse by highlighting neglected conditions that disproportionately affect women in endemic settings. Through policy engagement, knowledge translation, and interdisciplinary collaboration, the proposed framework will support more coordinated responses to intersecting health challenges.

Ultimately, the project envisions a health system where women at risk of FGS are not overlooked within broader reproductive health programs. By integrating screening pathways and strengthening policy alignment, Nigeria can improve early detection, reduce disease burden, and advance more gender-responsive and equitable health systems.
